Te Whare Tapa Whā - Wellbeing Plan

Taha Tinana (Physical Wellbeing)

Physical wellbeing is super important for keeping my energy up and my mind sharp, especially with all the focus coding requires. I already do a fair bit to keep myself moving and will continue working on this as part of my daily routine. I walk most days, often heading to the local coffee shop and back, which helps me reset. I also enjoy short walks to the shops, as they give me a little break from the screen. Occasionally, I plan to take my mom's dog for an evening walk to de-stress after a long day of work and study. Sleep is another non-negotiable; I'll aim for 7 hours a night to ensure I'm rested and ready for whatever challenges come my way.

Taha Whānau (Family Wellbeing)

For me, whanau isn't just about immediate family; it's about the people I share my life with. I prioritize having lunch and dinner every day with my partner, who I'm building my life with here in New Zealand. This daily connection allows us to support each other and stay close. I also aim to have weekly dinners with either my partner's family or my mom, as these gatherings help strengthen our bonds. Additionally, at Dev Academy and beyond, I'm committed to engaging with my peers, checking in with one another, and offering support when things get tough, knowing that this collective effort helps everyone stay on track.

Taha Hinengaro (Mental & Emotional Wellbeing)

Mental and emotional wellbeing is key to long-term success, especially in a challenging field like web development. I'm already making efforts to support my mental health and will continue to focus on it. For instance, I plan to spend 10 minutes each night doing guided meditation or deep breathing exercises to unwind after a long day of learning and coding. I'm also working on setting clear boundaries regarding work, study, and life to prevent burnout, which means no late-night coding marathons unless absolutely necessary, and making time for non-tech activities that help me recharge mentally.

Taha Wairua (Spiritual Wellbeing)

For me, taha wairua is about staying connected to my purpose and values, which give meaning to the work I do. I plan to take time each week to reflect on why I'm pursuing my goals and how they align with my bigger aspirations. Practicing gratitude daily is also important; I want to appreciate small wins, like finally fixing a tricky bug, and recognize the supportive people in my life. This practice helps me stay positive and connected to what matters most.

Whenua (Connection to the Land and Environment)

Living in New Zealand while being far from my homeland adds a significant layer to my understanding of whenua. Keeping that connection alive is really important to me, and visiting my home country in June with my partner was a special experience that reinforced my ties to where I come from. I love having little souvenirs from home — items that remind me of my roots and keep me connected to my culture. Even when I'm miles away, feeling that connection to the motherland is grounding, so I plan to take moments each week to walk barefoot on the grass. On nice days, I'll also take my laptop outside and code in the fresh air, blending nature with work to feel more connected to my surroundings.
